Rising Feminism

Late in the 19th century, with the unprecedented growth of industry, Victorian females began to be more conscious about their independence and equality than men. Then came the first wave of feminism which focused on the legal right and suffrage of women. At the same time, the New Woman Movement started to liberate social stereotypes about gender.

Remaining Patriarchy

This satire's most ingenious point is the mockery of Victorian norms. It depicts a host of educated females in the aristocracy stratum, seemingly independent and smart. And then Wilde gradually revealed the hypocrisy and absurdity in their nature, indicating that although the society had seen some revolutionary concepts, it was still managed under the fixed cliche aligned with patriarchy.
Oscar Wilde's satire not only entertains but also critiques the superficialness of Victorian society. Through the depiction of female characters, the play reveals the tension between traditional women's perceptions and emerging modernity.
The history shows the evolution of human will and the liberation of all females. Following the first tide of feminism come the second, the third and the fourth. The conception of gender equality, self-awareness and independence subsequently covers every aspects of our lives.

Cecily and Gwendolen

Either blanketed by innocence or adorned with nobility, they all fall in love with an unreal man called 'Ernest' against their guardians' will. Such behaviour may demonstrate their intelligence and independence but inevitably sinks into the marsh of ignorance and superficiality. By the sharp comparison between the moment before and after they've known the fact, it has been clarified that they merely disguise themselves to be pure and morally superior more or less. One way or another, Gwendolen's and Cecily's initiative of love truly embodies the rising women status and consciousness in that era.

Lady Bracknell

She seems to own the authority in marriage, while every judgements of her are originated from the perspective of ancestry and social background, suggesting she has alienated as the vindicator of norms formed in the patriarchism era.
